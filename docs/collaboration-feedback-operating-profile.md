# Collaboration & Feedback Operating Profile

**Profile:** Dave
**Date:** March 19, 2026
**Document:** 2 of 7

---

## Feedback Preferences

### Non-binary approach:

- Feedback is not always "right or wrong" — especially in construction, strategy, and forward-moving work
- Prefers: "Here's what I see" or "This might not be correct" over binary judgments
- Open to ideas, input, guidance, and learning experiences
- Challenge: People who see everything as binary right/wrong create friction

### What works:

- Nuanced, context-aware feedback
- Acknowledging that "correct" comes in different flavors
- Collaborative framing: "We think this might not work because..."

### What doesn't work:

- Overly pointed or judgmental tone
- Treating every issue as black-and-white

---

## Mistakes & Ownership

### Core philosophy:
Mistakes are fine. Hiding them is not.

### What matters:

1. **Own it** — Acknowledge the mistake openly
2. **Don't hide it** — Transparency over cover-ups
3. **Learn from it** — Repetitive mistakes signal a need for better tools, teaching, or understanding

### When mistakes repeat:

- Dave's first response: "Do they have the right tools to do the job?"
- Not carelessness — missing resources or clarity
- Solution: More teaching, better tools, deeper understanding

### Dealbreaker:

- Dishonesty about mistakes destroys collaboration
- Breaks trust, disengages from the team, signals unwillingness to succeed
- This is where Dave draws a hard line

### Grace period for new people:

- New engagements get a "lenient lens" — don't judge too quickly
- Need time to understand the pattern before making calls
- Long-term contributors get more benefit of the doubt if something shifts

## What Makes a Good Collaborator

### Key traits:

- **Takes things off your desk** — Reduces load instead of adding to it
- **Shares knowledge approachably** — Knowledgeable but easy to work with
- **Wants to learn** — Actively seeks growth and skill-building
- **Takes ownership** — Steps up without being asked

### What excites Dave:

When someone wants to do more, build their skills, and expand their knowledge — that energy is contagious and valued.

---

## What Causes Friction

### 1. False Expertise

- Someone presents themselves as knowledgeable when they're clearly not
- Dave can tell — his own knowledge reveals the gap
- Feels deceptive even if not intentional
- Creates a "minefield" dynamic — multiple paths to navigate, all tricky

### 2. Dishonesty

- Especially about mistakes in a non-punitive environment
- Dave explicitly fosters a "mistakes are learning" culture
- When people still hide mistakes, it signals fear or distrust
- Undermines the foundation of psychological safety Dave works to build

### Dave's awareness:

- Knows there may be fear of judgment even in a safe environment
- Tries to reinforce non-judgment explicitly
- But dishonesty remains a friction point that's hard to navigate

## Working Dynamics

### Context matters:

- **Individual collaboration** — One-on-one partnership
- **Team collaboration** — Group coordination
- **Leadership role** — Dave leading, others supporting
- **Support role** — Dave supporting leadership above him

### In a support role:

- Models what he'd want from someone supporting him
- Brings knowledge, takes ownership, doesn't add to the pile
- Tries to be helpful and proactive without creating more work

## Conflict & Disagreement

### Philosophy:
Not afraid of conflict. Learned to navigate it over time.

### Approach:

- Very direct, open communicator
- Has learned to soften bluntness over time (was too direct earlier in career)
- Can disarm conflict in multiple ways:
  - **Humor** — Diffuse tension
  - **Common ground** — Find shared goals
  - **Strategic failure** — Let someone try the wrong path if it won't hurt too much (teaching through experience)

### Growth area:

- Recognizes past tendency to be too blunt
- Actively works on softening delivery without losing directness

## Support & Being Supported

### Context:
This is a vulnerable area. Dave rarely felt supported throughout his life, so the concept itself is "a pain area" to think about.

### What support looks like for Dave:

- **Shared investment** — Working toward common goals together
- **Same energy** — Matching commitment and drive
- **Succeeding together** — Collective wins, not individual cheerleading

### What support is NOT:

- Not about emotional reassurance
- Not about cheerleading or rah-rah energy
- Not about someone doing things for him

## Deadlines & Pressure

### Deadlines work when they're logical:

- Clear consequences: "If you don't do this, here's what happens"
- Real constraints, not artificial milestones
- Grounded in the **triple constraint** (scope, time, resources)

### Deadlines don't work when they're illogical:

- Arbitrary targets for the sake of celebration
- Unreasonable timelines that break feasibility
- When someone imposes an impossible deadline, Dave will say: "Your triangle's top point (time) has fallen through the bottom bar — it's not a triangle anymore. It's not feasible."

### Response to logical deadlines:

- Sees them as a challenge to meet
- Motivated by real constraints and real wins
- Pressure doesn't bother him when it makes sense

### Response to illogical deadlines:

- Frustration and pushback
- Will explain why it's not feasible (often visually, with triangle diagrams)
- Requires recalibration of scope or resources to make time realistic

---

## Summary

Dave values **honesty, ownership, shared investment, and logical constraints**. He's patient with mistakes, intolerant of dishonesty, and motivated by real challenges grounded in feasibility. He's learned to soften his directness over time but remains a clear, direct communicator who prefers collaboration over hierarchy.
